    Predicting Forest Fire Size

    Fall 2020 | Machine Learning Course Project
    Tools: Python, Jupyter Notebook

    Wildfires have been running rampant in the West coast, causing tremendous amounts of damage to forestland, wildlife, and property. Using a Kaggle dataset with features including longitude, latitude, cause of fire, discovery and containment date, and fire size, my team of five set out to discover whether we could predict the fire size class from the other features.

    After a bit of data cleansing and creation of a fire duration variable, my team tackled a few different machine learning tactics, from neural networks to multiple regression.

    I applied gradient boosting, and initially found a 63% accuracy. I decided to reduce the number of fire size classes from 7 to 3 (small, medium, and large). The model’s accuracy improved to nearly 80% after fine tuning parameters to find the best learning rate, max_features, and max_depth! I applied cross-validation to ensure overfitting was not occurring.

    Upon closer inspection, however, I discovered that while the model performed well on small fires, it performed quite poorly on medium and large fires. This accuracy of 80% was weighted based on the large amount of small fires in the dataset. The features listed earlier were not sufficient to consistently predict fire size. In the future, adding weather variables and obtaining more data for larger fires would be helpful to train our model.

    For more details, visit our GitHub page!

    Exploring Spotify's Top Artists

    Fall 2020 | Information Visualization Course Project
    Tools: D3.js, JavaScript, HTML, CSS

    We chose to explore the top 2000 songs released on Spotify of all time (1956 to 2019) using a Kaggle dataset that extracted features like energy, danceability, valence, accousticness, beats per minute (BPM), and many more from the Spotify API.


    The main bar chart is a ranking of the most popular artists ordered by the number of songs an artist produced that broke the top 2000. We linked the visualizations on the right to show details for a selected artist. The top right bar chart updates to show all of the selected artist’s songs in the top 2000, in descending order by popularity. The parallel coordinate plot below shows each song as a line, allowing users to view song features and compare the music of a single artist. Hovering over a line shows a tooltip with the song name.

    Our team enjoyed exploring the top artists and their songs on Spotify while creating this visualization. This was my first experience using D3.js, and I think I’m hooked by the simplicity and beauty of creating data visualizations with the JavaScript library.     Carbon Reduction Challenge

    Summer 2020 | Personal project during my internship at UPS
    Tools: Microsoft Excel, Adobe Photoshop, Canva

    I was thrilled to work at a company with a dedicated sustainability department. My role in Healthcare IT was far from carbon reporting, but I seized an opportunity to work with Georgia Tech faculty and identify areas of carbon reduction at UPS. I determined two main areas of carbon reduction (and cost savings), and presented these findings to UPS Sustainability.


    Work from Home (WFH)

    I sent out and analyzed two surveys on the commute of UPS employees, and calculated the carbon emissions savings from telecommuting. With information including car year, make, and model, daily commute mileage on highway and city roads, and WFH behavior, I captured the carbon and cost savings for Atlanta-based UPSers. Most employees surveyed preferred WFH 4 days/week post-COVID, which I recommended after modeling the savings for other WFH models. This structure best captures co-benefits of avoiding a commute and still offers weekly office collaboration. A fun find from my survey was that the majority of surveyed workers experienced no change or better productivity and performance working from home.


    Promote UPS.com Ground Shipping

    On UPS.com, customers can enter information on package volume, origin, and destination to ship their items. The default shipping option is air, which is costly for customers and produces much more carbon than ground shipping. As a step to reduce emissions across UPS operations, ground shipping should be denoted as “Most Sustainable” and/or be the recommended option. Below is a mockup I created of the website.

    I earned an Honorable Mention for my research and calculations from the Carbon Reduction Challenge judges, and got to learn from/connect with many UPSers who value sustainability in operations!

    LunchLinks

    Fall 2020 | Human-Computer Interaction Course Project
    Tools: Figma, Miro

    My team of five tackled the issue of lack of social interaction during the COVID-19 pandemic. It was my first time undergoing the typical design process, as outlined below. We wanted to bring back “face-to-face” meals under the guise of professional networking. Our project encouraged human interaction over meals and focused on the mentorship aspect of career-building. The result of our semester-long research and iterative design process? Enter LunchLinks: a LinkedIn-like networking site where in order to connect with someone, you must share a meal with them over a video call.

    Our primary stakeholders were students and working professionals. We surveyed, interviewed, and observed our stakeholder groups to understand the issue better. Following this research portion, we created personas and developed design criteria.

    "People are able to easily engage in work or entertainment when they eat alone."

    Engaging Meals

    "I think the most important part for a healthy student life would probably be being socially connected with other people."

    Social/Mental Health Benefits

    "Eating with others makes it easier to network because people put their guard down."

    Networking Professionally

    "It’s nice to see what people do outside of work/school."

    Casual Conversations

    We came up with 100+ ideas to address our design criteria and separated them into four categories. We discussed and voted on three final solutions.

    I created storyboards to describe the ways each solution could be used by our stakeholders. An idea that we didn’t end up using was “Quarantine Chefs”, which I show below along with a storyboard for LunchLinks, the idea we continued building on.

    In our final prototyping stage, we created low-fidelity wireframes in Figma. We practiced heuristic evaluation to identify usability bugs and design criteria violations in our prototype, and made preparations for a final stakeholder evaluation to cap off our project.



    LunchLinks: Bring back the social aspect of eating meals through networking.

    World Happiness Index

    Spring 2020 | Regression/Forecasting Course Project
    Tools: R, Minitab

    What determines if an individual is happy or not? Though happiness is not quantifiable, the World Happiness Report attempts to create a “happiness index” which ranks countries based on independent variables like health, ecological diversity and resilience, psychological well being, community vitality, living standard, and education.

    My team decided to create a model with a different set of independent variables from the World Happiness Report to predict the happiness index of a country. Using World Bank's DataBank and various other online databases, we gathered data for 15 development indicators (e.g. GDP, education expenditure, life expectancy) in a total of 138 countries.

    We used backward elimination, stepwise regression, and best subsets to determine a model that passed assumptions checks and residual analysis. We also tried adding higher order and interaction terms to identify the best model.

    I performed analysis in R to find outliers, high leverage points, and influential points that could be affecting our chosen model. 17 countries were outliers with respect to their x and/or y values and/or were influential. We decided only to remove the country Saudi Arabia because it was influential according to its Cook’s Distance and Difference in Fits values and outlying with respect to both its x and y value.

    After removing the outlier, we produced our final model with stepwise regression and squared terms. The normality and independence assumptions were met. Finally, we tested the accuracy of our model prediction on a random sample of 5 countries separate from the training set. With a mean absolute percentage error (MAPE) of 9.237%, we believe that our model is reliable in predicting the happiness index for most countries.




    I learned how to familiarize myself with R for a project that didn't start out with clean data or a definite answer waiting at the end of my calculations. It was my first time going through an iterative process to find the "best" answer. Though our final model met all the error assumptions, we certainly would have benefited from more data. In the future, we could incorporate a time study to identify how a country's happiness index fluctuates through the years. Also, the Happiness Report is a really interesting read and worth checking it out!
